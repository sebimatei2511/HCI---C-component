1. Observe the existing affordances, visual variables and interaction flows for a given use case

Discord:

  • Discord offers voice and video chat features, screen sharing, live streaming options, and the ability to upload and share files. However, it lacks presentation tools such as a slideshow feature.
  • Discord has the option for users to create and join servers and channels to chat with multiple people.
  • Discord has a sleek and modern interface with customizable themes. The chat window shows a list of online members, and their status is indicated by colored dots.

Skype:

  • Skype offers video and audio calls, screen sharing, and file sharing. It also has a built-in presentation mode that allows users to share their screen and run a slideshow. However, it lacks the ability to annotate or highlight specific parts of the presentation.
  • Skype users can chat with multiple people by creating and joining groups.
  • Skype has a simple interface with a list of contacts and a chat window. The chat window shows the conversation history and displays the participants' profile pictures.

Attending a meeting at late hours:

Discord:

  • Discord has a dark mode, which can help reduce eye strain during late hours. However, it lacks the ability to schedule meetings and send reminders.

Skype:

  • Skype also has a dark mode and allows users to schedule meetings in advance. It also has a notification system to remind users of upcoming meetings.

Interaction Flows:

On both applications the users can change their status so the other users can check the availability of other users.

Overall, both Skype and Discord offer similar features for communication, but their interaction flows differ in terms of how you initiate conversations and join groups. Skype focuses more on individual contacts, while Discord emphasizes community building through servers and channels.

2. Compare the visual metaphors and idioms and suggest the similar ones for a text-only interaction

Discord and Skype are both graphical user interfaces (GUI) that use visual metaphors and idioms to create a familiar and intuitive experience for users. In contrast, a Command Line Interface (CLI) is a text-only interface that relies on command input and output to interact with the system. Here are some comparisons between the visual metaphors and idioms used in Discord and Skype and suggestions for similar ones for a text-only interaction:

Discord:

  • Metaphor: "Server" and "Channel" are used to organize and group conversations and users.
  • Idiom: Colored dots next to user's names indicate their status (online, idle, do not disturb, etc.).
  • Suggestion for CLI: Use a simple folder structure to organize and group commands and responses. Use a prompt that indicates the user's status.

Skype:

  • Metaphor: "Contacts" and "Groups" are used to organize and group conversations and users.
  • Idiom: Green checkmark next to a contact's name indicates that they are online.
  • Suggestion for CLI: Use a simple list of commands and responses to organize and group conversations and users. Use a prompt that indicates whether the user is online or offline.

To summarize, visual metaphors and idioms are not relevant for a text-only interface such as a CLI. However, it is possible to use simple folder structures and lists of commands and responses to arrange and group users and conversations. To provide additional context, prompts can be utilized to convey the user's availability to receive messages.

3. Discuss the cognitive overload

Cognitive overload occurs when a person is presented with too much information or too many tasks to process at once, leading to a decrease in productivity and an increase in errors. This can be particularly problematic in special conditions or contexts, such as delivering an academic presentation or attending a specific meeting at late hours, where individuals may already be experiencing additional stress or fatigue. Here are some ways in which cognitive overload may impact the use of Discord and Skype in these special conditions:

Delivering an academic presentation:

  • Cognitive overload can occur if the presenter is trying to manage too many channels or conversations at once, leading to missed messages or lost focus.
  • To reduce cognitive overload, the presenter may want to limit the number of channels they are monitoring during the presentation, or assign a moderator to manage the conversations for them.

Attending a specific meeting at late hours:

  • Cognitive overload can occur if the participant is already experiencing fatigue, making it more difficult to process information and communicate effectively.
  • To reduce cognitive overload, the participant may want to limit their use of visual elements, such as emojis or animated GIFs, which can be distracting and add to cognitive load. Additionally, using keyboard shortcuts and other productivity tools can help reduce the time it takes to respond to messages, reducing the overall cognitive load.

Overall, in special conditions or contexts where cognitive overload may be a concern, it is important to simplify the user interface and limit distractions as much as possible to reduce cognitive load. Additionally, using productivity tools and assigning moderators to manage conversations can help manage cognitive load and improve productivity.

5. How the empiric laws (Fitts’, Hick’s, Steering) might be reconsidered/adapted in the context of multi-device interaction?

Fitts' law predicts the pointing (movement) average time as a function of the distance to and width of the given target. In a multi-device interaction, the distance to the target may vary depending on the device being used, and the size of the target may also differ. Therefore, it may be necessary to consider the relative distance and size of targets across different devices to accurately predict the time required to complete a task.

Hick's law states that the time required to make a decision increases with the number of alternatives. In a multi-device interaction, the number of alternatives may also vary depending on the device being used. For example, a desktop computer may have more options and features available than a mobile device. Therefore, it may be necessary to account for the differences in the number of alternatives across devices to accurately predict the time required to make a decision.

Steering law predicting the time of moving the hand through a “tunnel” having the length D and width S. In a multi-device interaction, the empirical Steering law needs to be adapted by accounting for factors such as device switching time, input modality, and screen size and resolution. For example, if a user is working on a task that requires using Discord on a laptop and Skype on a mobile device, the empirical Steering law needs to consider the additional time it takes for the user to switch between the two devices.


Overall, it is important to consider the unique characteristics of each device and the context in which it is being used when applying the empirical laws of Fitts', Hick's, and Steering to multi-device interaction. By doing so, designers can create interfaces that are optimized for different devices and provide a seamless user experience across devices.
